The Q cycle in the cytochrome b₆f complex is a sophisticated proton-motive mechanism that enhances the efficiency of photosynthetic electron transport in plants. Here's a detailed description of its mechanism and significance:

Role of the Cytochrome b₆f Complex

  • Location: Embedded in the thylakoid membrane of chloroplasts.
  • Function: Transfers electrons from Photosystem II (PSII) to Photosystem I (PSI) via plastoquinol (PQH₂) and plastocyanin (PC).
  • Key Outcome: Pumps protons (H⁺) into the thylakoid lumen, contributing to the electrochemical gradient driving ATP synthesis.

Summary of the Q Cycle

Proton & Electron Accounting

  • Per PQH₂ oxidized at Qo:
    • 2 H⁺ released into the lumen.
    • 1 electron sent to PSI (via PC).
    • 1 electron sent to cytochrome b₆.
  • Per cycle (2 PQH₂ oxidized):
    • 4 H⁺ pumped into the lumen.
    • 2 electrons sent to PSI.
    • 1 PQH₂ regenerated at Qᵢ (using 2 H⁺ from stroma).
  • Net Result: 4 H⁺ translocated per 2 electrons (doubling H⁺/e⁻ ratio).

Key Features

  • Quinone Bifurcation: PQH₂ oxidation splits electrons into high- and low-energy paths.
  • Asymmetric Sites: Qo (lumen) and Qi (stroma) enable cross-membrane proton movement.
  • Dimeric Structure: Two identical monomers share substrates, enhancing efficiency.
  • Proton Gradient Amplification: Critical for ATP synthesis in photosynthesis.

Biological Significance

  • Energy Efficiency: Maximizes ATP yield per electron for carbon fixation (Calvin cycle).
  • Regulation: Sensitive to pH, redox state, and inhibitors (e.g., antimycin A blocks Qi).
  • Evolution: Conserved from cytochrome bc₁ in mitochondria, optimized for photosynthesis.
